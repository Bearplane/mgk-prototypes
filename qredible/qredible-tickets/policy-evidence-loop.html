📜 POLICY-FIRST ARCHITECTURE — This page proposes the closed-loop system tying scan findings BACK to policy assignment. Q-ScanAI findings, product scans, COA results, license scans, OFAC/MRI checks, and payment rail detection all produce evidence tags; policies declare expected evidence signatures; the coverage check detects when a merchant has evidence of a regulated activity that no assigned policy covers — and surfaces the gap as a suggested policy.

Policy Evidence Loop — Detect Missing Policies from Real Evidence

The merchant's policy stack should reflect what they actually do, not just what they said at sign-up. Companion to Policy Editor, Bulletin Authoring, and Policy Lifecycle.

1 The Big Idea

Policies define what we look for. Findings tell us what we found. If we find evidence of a regulated activity that none of the merchant's assigned policies cover, that's a coverage gap — and the system should catch it automatically and suggest (or auto-attach) the missing policy.

2 Sources of Evidence

The platform already has rich scan and check infrastructure. Each scan type produces facts about the merchant that can be normalized into evidence tags.

🔍 Q-ScanAI website findings
Keyword detections, page-level violations, missing disclaimers, prohibited-state shipping detection.
🔍 Product scans (Project Z)
Catalog item titles, descriptions, ingredients, claims, categories. Pulled via Zyte against the merchant's e-commerce surfaces.
🔍 COA results (Project Cambridge)
Analyte values, lab IDs, batch numbers, presence of specific cannabinoids/alkaloids extracted by Aluma.
🔍 License scans (CannaSpyGlass)
License types currently active, license states, expiration dates, license-holder name reconciliation.
🔍 OFAC / MRI checks
Sanctions hits, PEP matches, BBB grades, court records, FDA warning letters, FTC actions.
🔍 Payment rail detection
Checkout payment methods detected on the live site — cards, ACH, crypto, P2P apps like Venmo/Cashapp/Zelle.
🔍 Domain & business identity
WHOIS data, address consistency across listings, DBA reconciliation, contact identity verification.

Each finding gets stamped with one or more evidence tags — short, normalized facts about what was observed. The tag vocabulary is the bridge between raw scan output and policy logic.

Each tag is consumed by policies in two ways: (a) by the policies the merchant ALREADY has, to evaluate compliance; and (b) by the coverage check, to detect MISSING policies. The vocabulary is fixed/extensible (a small schema, code-driven), not a separate admin surface.

4 Coverage Check — Detecting Missing Policies

Each canonical policy declares an "expected evidence signature" — the set of evidence tags that, if observed on a merchant, mean the merchant should be subject to this policy.

Example signature — Policy kratom-v1 declares its signature as:
contains-kratom-keyword analyte-detected-mitragynine analyte-detected-7-hydroxymitragynine product-category-kratom

Any one of those tags appearing on a merchant = strong signal they should be subject to kratom-v1.

01For each evidence tag t observed on merchant m:
02 Find all canonical policies P whose signature includes t
03 Compare P against m.policy_ids
04 If a matching policy exists in the library but is NOT assigned → COVERAGE GAP
05 Open a "Suggested Policy" item in the Q-Audit review queue with:
• the suggested policy_id
• the supporting evidence tags + their source findings
• a confidence score (see Section 6)

6 Confidence Levels & Auto-Attach Thresholds

Not every coverage gap should auto-attach. Confidence is a function of how many distinct evidence sources support the suggestion.

HIGH — 3+ sources

Multiple distinct scan types produced matching evidence (e.g. keyword + product + COA). Auto-attach + notify merchant.

MEDIUM — 2 sources or 1 strong

Two related sources or one high-signal source (active license, COA analyte). Suggest in review queue. Analyst decides.

LOW — 1 weak source

A single keyword hit or a single ambiguous tag. Log only. Aggregate over time; surface if it crosses thresholds later.

7 Detecting Policies That No Longer Apply 🔁

The reverse case is just as real. A merchant on tobacco-vape-v1 who removes all vape products from their catalog should eventually have that policy detached.

The argument: yes, but with a longer evidence window (e.g., zero matching evidence across 90 days of scans), and probably suggest-only, never auto-detach. Removing compliance coverage is risk-asymmetric — if the auto-attach side is wrong, you've added some checklist items the merchant doesn't need; if the auto-detach side is wrong, you've stopped checking something that legally applies. Default to caution.

8 Before / After 🧭

What this changes about the platform's relationship with the merchant.

OLD model — industry-keyed, no feedback loop

Merchant tells you what they sell at sign-up → you trust them → scans run with hardcoded checks → findings produce flags → no feedback loop → policy never adapts. If they pivot or expand into a new category, you find out from a stakeholder complaint or a missed compliance event.

NEW model — policy-first + evidence loop

Merchant tells you what they sell at sign-up → assigned policies → scans run with policy-driven checks → findings produce evidence tags → coverage check runs after every scan → coverage gaps surface as suggested policies → analyst reviews → policies attach → merchant's policy stack auto-converges on what they actually do.

9 Open Questions ⚠️

The decision-forcing items specific to the evidence loop. These need alignment before this ships.

Auto-attach vs suggest-only thresholdsWhere exactly does the line sit? Brian/Ronaldo decision. Default conservative posture: HIGH confidence still routes to 1-click analyst confirmation; nothing fully auto-attaches in v1.
Evidence tag vocabulary governanceWho owns the canonical tag list? How does it evolve when a new regulated category emerges? Is there a tag-add review process or is it dev-only?
False positive challenge flowHow does a merchant challenge a wrongly-suggested policy? "We don't actually sell kratom — that's a CBD product line called Kratom Kalm." Needs a flag-and-explain workflow into Q-Audit.
Reverse case (detach) — keep or punt for v1?Risk-asymmetric vs the attach case. Probably ship attach first, validate, add detach later with longer evidence windows.
Cross-merchant signal — long-tail policy discoveryIf many merchants in the same MCC consistently produce evidence of a regulated activity that no canonical policy covers yet, should the system suggest creating a new policy? Definitely v2+, but worth flagging now so the data model doesn't preclude it.
SOC 2 audit trailEvery coverage decision needs to be auditable: which evidence triggered it, who reviewed, what action was taken, when. Probably the same audit log as policy publication, scoped to coverage events.
Performance & cadenceHow often does the coverage check run? After every scan (real-time but expensive), batched nightly (cheap but slow), or per-merchant on a separate cadence? Default: run after every completed scan, batched at policy-library granularity.
